About the Translator

George Webbe Dasent (1817⁠–⁠1896) was a distinguished British scholar, lawyer, and diplomat whose lifelong fascination with Northern European culture left an indelible mark on Victorian literature. Educated at King’s College London and Oxford, he spent several years in Stockholm as a secretary to the British legation, an experience that ignited his passion for Scandinavian folklore and history. This deep immersion in the North allowed him to become one of the foremost translators of his era, bridging the gap between Icelandic sagas and the English-speaking world.

Best known for his seminal translation of Burnt Njal, Dasent played a pivotal role in introducing the epic grandeur of Icelandic literature to a wider audience. His extensive body of work includes the celebrated Popular Tales from the Norse, which remains a cornerstone of folklore studies. Beyond his literary achievements, he served as a prominent contributor to The Times for over twenty-five years and held the position of Civil Service Commissioner, cementing his reputation as a versatile intellectual and a vital cultural mediator of the nineteenth century.
